What is Quantum Computing

Computing at the edge of physical reality

Classical computers (the one running this page, the one in your phone) work with bits. A bit is always either 0 or 1. Every calculation your laptop has ever done, every video streamed, every message sent, came down to billions of these binary switches flipping on and off.

Quantum computers work differently. Instead of bits, they use qubits and qubits exploit the strange rules of quantum mechanics to do something a classical bit never can: exist in a superposition of 0 and 1 simultaneously, until you look at them.

This isn't magic, it's physics. Quantum mechanics, the theory describing how particles behave at atomic scales, tells us that particles don't have definite states until measured. A qubit exploits this: before measurement, it's genuinely in both states at once, with some probability of being 0 and some probability of being 1.

Quantum computing doesn't make computers faster at the same tasks. It changes the nature of what's computable at all.

Two more quantum phenomena make this powerful: entanglement (where two qubits become correlated so measuring one instantly affects the other, no matter how far apart) and interference (where quantum states can cancel or amplify each other like waves). Clever algorithms engineer these effects so the right answer becomes overwhelmingly probable when you measure.

My Journey

Starting from Electronics, landing in quantum

I study Electronics and Telecommunication Engineering. My days are full of signal processing, Fourier transforms, transfer functions, and circuit analysis. I have also coded Python and ML experiments on the side. But I always had a keen interest in Quantum Physics.

Quantum physics always fascinated me. Even before I understood the math, there was something compelling about a universe that behaves this strangely at small scales — particles in superposition, entanglement defying intuition, measurement collapsing reality. It felt like science fiction that happened to be true. So when I discovered that quantum computing is essentially applied quantum physics; that those same strange phenomena can be engineered into algorithms that solve problems no classical computer can; the transition felt inevitable. Not a pivot, more like a convergence. My foundation gave me the math,my curiosity about quantum physics gave me the motivation. Quantum computing sits exactly at that intersection.

I expected quantum computing to feel alien. What surprised me was how much of it I already knew — just under different names.

Core Concepts

The ideas you need to actually understand QC

Most introductions either drown you in physics or stay so abstract you can't do anything. Here's what I found to be the minimum viable set of concepts — the ones that actually unlock the algorithms.

🌊

Superposition

A qubit exists in both |0⟩ and |1⟩ simultaneously until measured. The Hadamard gate creates this equal superposition from |0⟩.

🔗

Entanglement

Two qubits become correlated — measuring one instantly determines the other. Created with H + CNOT. The source of quantum's power.

〰️

Interference

Quantum amplitudes add and cancel like waves. Algorithms engineer constructive interference on correct answers, destructive on wrong ones.

📏

Measurement

Observing a qubit collapses superposition to 0 or 1 probabilistically. Probability = |amplitude|². The art is maximizing the right answer's probability before measuring.

What Qiskit Code Actually Looks Like

# Bell State: the simplest entangled state from qiskit import QuantumCircuit from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator from qiskit.visualization import plot_histogram # Build circuit: 2 qubits, 2 classical bits qc = QuantumCircuit(2, 2) qc.h(0) # Hadamard: put qubit 0 in superposition qc.cx(0, 1) # CNOT: entangle qubit 0 and qubit 1 qc.measure([0,1], [0,1]) # Simulate and visualize counts = AerSimulator().run(qc, shots=1000).result().get_counts() plot_histogram(counts) # Output: ~500x '00', ~500x '11' — never '01' or '10' # The qubits are perfectly correlated. That's entanglement.

Resources for Beginners

Free resources that helped me learn

Here's what I found genuinely useful as an engineering student starting from zero.

Video

Quantum Computing — A Very Good Overall Introduction (YouTube)

The best single video to watch before anything else. Builds genuine intuition for what quantum computing actually is before you touch any math or code. Start here, before the papers.

Paper

A Minimal Introduction to Quantum Computing — arXiv:2504.00995

Written for ML/programming folks. No physics required. Gets you to Deutsch-Jozsa in 20 pages. Start here.

Paper

Quantum Computing Without the Physics — arXiv:1708.03684 (Nannicini)

Short, mathematical, CS-focused. Covers Grover's and Simon's algorithm with full circuit details.

Paper

Quantum Algorithm Implementations for Beginners — arXiv:1804.03719

Surveys 20 algorithms with pseudocode and circuit diagrams. Includes IBM hardware implementation tips.

Tool

Qiskit + IBM Quantum (quantum.ibm.com)

Free Python library for quantum circuits. Run on simulator locally or on real quantum hardware via IBM cloud. Start with Aer simulator.

Tool

Google Colab

Zero setup. Install Qiskit with one pip command, run everything in browser. Best way to start today without any configuration pain.

Course

IBM Quantum Learning (learning.quantum.ibm.com)

Structured labs that complement the papers. Free. Goes from basics to real hardware. Best complement to self-study.

Why This Matters

The future quantum computing is building toward

Quantum computing isn't a solution looking for a problem. There are specific, well-defined classes of problems where quantum algorithms provide proven advantages over the best known classical algorithms. These aren't marginal improvements — they're qualitative leaps.

💊

Drug Discovery

Simulating molecular interactions at quantum scale is classically intractable. Quantum computers can model drug-protein binding directly, potentially cutting drug development timelines from decades to years.

🔐

Cryptography

Shor's algorithm breaks RSA encryption. Every government and company is preparing for "Q-Day." Quantum-safe cryptography standards (NIST PQC) are being deployed now.

⚡

Optimization

Logistics, supply chains, financial portfolios, traffic routing — these are NP-hard classically. Quantum annealing and QAOA offer promising paths to approximate solutions at scale.

🤖

Quantum ML

Quantum kernels and variational circuits can potentially process high-dimensional data exponentially more efficiently. Active research area at every major tech lab.

🌡️

Climate & Materials

Designing new materials for solar cells, batteries, and carbon capture requires quantum simulation. The molecules are quantum systems — simulate them quantumly.

💹

Finance

Monte Carlo simulations for risk analysis, portfolio optimization, and derivative pricing. Quantum amplitude estimation provides quadratic speedup over classical Monte Carlo.

We are at the 1950s moment of quantum computing. The people learning this now are the ones who will build what comes next.

The hardware is still noisy and limited — we're in the NISQ era (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum). But the algorithms exist, the theory is solid, and engineering the hardware is a relentless engineering problem being attacked by IBM, Google, IonQ, QuEra, and dozens of startups with billions in funding. The question isn't if but when — and being technically literate now is the leverage point.
